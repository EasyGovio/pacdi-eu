What to Watch for When Working with AI

AI tools produce fast, polished answers — but fluency is not proof of accuracy. This guide explains what an AI hallucination is and what to watch for when using AI output with confidence.

PACDI · Current

What is an AI hallucination?

A hallucination happens when an AI model invents information it doesn't actually know or couldn't access, in a way that sounds convincing. The model isn't trying to deceive — it simply predicts the next most likely word, and can produce a fluent, confident sentence even without a real source behind it. A professional-sounding answer is therefore no guarantee of factual accuracy.

When a date, company name, legal clause, or statistic appears — especially if the AI claims to have "read" a link or document — it's often impossible to tell whether it genuinely came from that source or is a plausible guess. Verification is the only reliable answer.

Why does this matter?

For everyday questions, the cost of a hallucination is usually small. But when the topic involves health, legal, or financial matters, or an urgent decision, a wrong date or a fabricated figure can have real consequences. Treating AI output as a starting point rather than the sole basis for a critical decision is a healthy habit.

How to verify in practice

A short checklist

  • Look up any name, date, or figure independently (official website, search engine).
  • If the AI claims to have read a link or document, explicitly ask whether it actually had access.
  • Notice hedge words like "generally" or "likely" — these can signal the model isn't certain.
  • Ask the same question again, phrased differently, and check whether the answer stays consistent.
  • For critical topics, use AI as a starting point — have the final decision confirmed by an expert or official source.

Conclusion

Used well, AI is a genuinely valuable tool — it speeds up research, drafts content, and helps you think from different angles. But it should be treated as an assistant, not an authority. The healthiest approach is simple: read the output, use it — but verify before acting on anything important.
